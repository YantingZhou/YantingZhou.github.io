FLORA YANTING ZHOU

Space mining game 

Time: April. 2022
Course: Creative Technology 2
Instructor: Maxim Safioulline
Tool: Processing, Arduino, ESP32, Accelerometer, Potentiometer

Project Brief:

BUILDING FOR PLAY

In this project we will explore the technical components of a video-based game: the physics and mechanics expressed in code, and the physical-to-digital connection from the game controller to the screen. You will have to consider goals and motivations for the players, the scoring system, the narrative, the art and atmosphere, and the physical aspects of play embodied in the ergonomic concerns. While complex, the result need not be complcated - but fun is mandatory! The outcome of the project will be an original video game and a custom controller for it.

DESIGN PROCESS

Source of Inspiration

  • My first inspiration is the tunnel ball adventure I played on iPad when little.
  • No Man Sky became my midway inspiration. It is one of my favorite game.  Especially shooting at the space rocks and gaining minerals.

Sketching the Idea

  • Initially I intended to design a space parkour game
  • The player needs to move left and right (rotation) to avoid the enemies appearing in the tunnel.

Building the Game Scene

  • First, the tunnel. I found a tutorial for increasing geometric shapes that switch between circles and squares.
  • I reduced the transparency of the background slightly to create the impression of weaving through the tunnel. Then combined it with the code from the running game in class.
  • Obstacles are very similar to tunnels in mechanism, both appearing and zooming in from visual vanishing points. So I made the obstacles by referring the tunnel, the difference is that the position of obstacle have a vector acceleration from the center point.
  • In the class, I got help from the instructor about how to add obstacles. If a random value from 0-1 is less than 0.03, then an obstacle is generated. This means that every draw loop has a 3% chance of adding an obstacle.
  • Once the scene visualization had been built, I started working on the collision calculation. This was complicated because my ship and the obstacle were not in the same axes.
  • I drew some reference objects. The white circles represent the coordinates corresponding to the obstacles I was trying to find in the ship's axes. This uses trigonometry. But the circles are not synchronized with the obstacles, indicating that there is a problem in calculating the angle.
  • The ball ocationally dies (turn to black), that means it did have collison but there is problem in position as well.

Calculating of the Rotation Angle

  • To calculate the dynamic rotation angle, I spent a long time sketching. At first only the matrix of the obstacle was translated, resulting in two matrices with different origins. This is almost insurmountable.
  • The next day I came up an idea to translate the matrix of the spaceship to midpoints too, making the two matrices at least have the same origins.
    This is the right path. I calculated the angle and distance within the for lop of enemy in the rotating matrix, and use them out of the pushmatrix() and popmatrix(). Therefore, I got the global cordinate of enemy.
  • It's worth noting that although I got the coordinates, I could only get the last coordinate in the obstacle loop at a time due to the limitations of the for loop. This means that there can only be one obstacle position in the screen that is valid. So this is a key point, I changed the rules of the game, the ship from avoiding "obstacles" to collecting "obstacles" (the only valid position).

Polishing the Game Scene

Spaceship Design

  • I changed the white circle to golden star and designed the shape of the ship. I set it up so that when the spaceship collides with a star, it scores points.

Conneting the Joysticks

  • The accelerometer on the left hand is to control the direction of the spaceship. Players can tilt it up-down and right-left;
    The potentiometer (knob) for the right hand is for the rotation of the view. 

Making the Enclosure

  • At first I planned to use a bottle to cover the accelerometer to make the form of a console.
  • In playtesting, I found that the bottle blocked the player from controlling the direction, so I removed the sensor and covered it with a direct wrap.

Playtesting

  • The test help me iterate my console.

Insights gained from the project

Whether I was testing my own work or trying out a classmate's work, I experienced a variety of designed interactions. 
My work experimented with more innovative ways of interacting, which can be a little difficult for some players to get used to. It dawned on me that there are conventions in interaction design that actually take the pressure off the user to learn. Designing new ways to interact can sometimes be risky, but there are also opportunities to make the interaction fun.

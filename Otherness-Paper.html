From Art to Technology:
Abstraction of the Orient Others in Western Context 

Time: Sep. 2021
Course: Critical History 1
Instructor: Arden Stern

Research Question 
We live in an intricate world of power, a place of inequity. All the “ism” we encountered from Orientalism, Racism, to Sexism seems to have a common ground of isolation from one subject to the others. The suppression, the drive of desire and benefits, maybe there is more.
The historical impact of colonialism has woven into modern society, causing social injustice includes stereotypes and misunderstanding of other cultures, which inevitably lies in each production of human society. Same in the art world, from the century-old great artworks of genius individuals to contemporary design practices, the distortion embedded. Such as the portraits of the middle-east and “primitive” tribes, in which the artists claim exoticism with their abstraction of the truth.
Nowadays, there are increasingly design attempts whether technological innovative or problem-solving. Looking inside these practices, we probably would still find the Otherness. Times changed, but discrimination has the potential to be deepened, with the iteration of technology. The ideology of white supremacy and colorblindness has been rooted in society, even the development of algorithms. From historical truth to art and design, I'm curious to find the internal association in between.
How is the Others abstracted in art and design in Western context? For this question, I would like to start with a series of case studies sorted by time in history. By analyzing the form, content, and context, I will look at the abstraction of the Other in works of art and design. Then I want to focus on positive cases that revolve around human-centered design and decolonizing design, sharing my insight and discussing questions like: In which way can a designer avoids being blinded by universal truth or take things for granted? How do the designers incline to the manifesto of decolonizing design as the principle of practice?
